# A Global Theory Attempt for Proposal-Based Counterfactual Intervention (CIC)

This document attempts to upgrade the CIC recovery theory from a *text-overlay-specific*
mechanism account into a **shortcut-type-agnostic** theorem for proposal-based
counterfactual intervention, while staying mathematically honest.

The result is **not** an assumption-free universal theorem — we prove that no such
theorem can exist for finite-query black-box methods (Section 6, the no-free-lunch
result). Instead we obtain a clean *conditional* structure:

> **The positive guarantee is shortcut-type-agnostic under proposal completeness and
> a class-balanced residual condition. The negative theorem shows why assumption-free
> open-world shortcut discovery is impossible for finite black-box intervention
> methods. CIC therefore occupies a principled middle ground: it is not universal
> discovery, but it is a general intervention framework whenever the candidate
> proposals cover the shortcut.**

This generalizes — it does not replace — the conditional finite-candidate recovery
theory in [`docs/theory.md`](theory.md). The per-input residual-to-clean recovery
corollary proved there (Section 9 of `theory.md`) is the recovery half of the
positive theorem below; the new content is (i) decoupling *existence* of a good
intervention from *selection* of it, (ii) making the statement family-agnostic, (iii)
the observable-margin gate, and (iv) the impossibility result that pins down why the
finite-candidate restriction is fundamental rather than incidental.

A small machine-checkable encoding of the core inequalities lives in
[`causal_reliability/theory/proposal_completeness.py`](../causal_reliability/theory/proposal_completeness.py)
with tests in
[`tests/test_proposal_completeness_theory.py`](../tests/test_proposal_completeness_theory.py).

## 1. General structural setting

A labelled input is generated by a structural process

```
x = g(c, s, n),    y* = h(c)
```

* `c` — **causal content** (the true object/shape that defines the label),
* `s` — a **shortcut factor** of *arbitrary type* (text overlay, icon/decoy, watermark,
  background, texture, metadata token, ...),
* `n` — **nuisance** variation (placement jitter, decoys, background detail),
* `h` — the true labelling function, depending only on `c`,
* `f` — a fixed black-box classifier returning per-class logits `ℓ_y(x)` (or
  probabilities `softmax`), with predicted class `argmax_y ℓ_y(x)`.

An **intervention** `T_a` is indexed by a candidate/proposal `a` drawn from a
**proposal family** `A(x)`. `T_a(x)` is a label-preserving edit (in the controlled
benchmarks: neutralize a region; replace a shortcut token; recolor a background). CIC
scores each candidate by a prediction-instability functional `score(a; x, f)`
(how much `f` changes under `T_a`) and selects the top-ranked candidate(s) for repair.
The scorer sees only pixels/tokens, candidate geometry, class prompts, and model
predictions — never the label, the shortcut identity, or correctness.

Notation reused from `theory.md`: let `x_clean` be the shortcut-free input with the
same causal content `c`, `m_clean(x) = ℓ_{y*}(x_clean) - max_{y≠y*} ℓ_y(x_clean)` the
clean causal margin, and `y* = argmax_y ℓ_y(x_clean)` the clean causal prediction.
(The benchmarks supply `x_clean` as the no-overlay image; at general deployment time
it need not be observable — see the gate, Section 5.)

This setting is deliberately *shortcut-type-agnostic*: nothing below names "text". The
type of `s` enters only through whether `A(x)` happens to contain a good intervention.

## 2. Proposal completeness

The single structural assumption that makes a family-agnostic theorem possible is that
the proposal family actually *contains* an intervention that fixes the shortcut without
breaking the content. We define it relative to the clean/causal logits.

For a candidate `a`, write the **residual-to-clean**

```
ρ_y^a(x) = ℓ_y(T_a(x)) - ℓ_y(x_clean),    ρ̄^a(x) = (1/|Y|) Σ_y ρ_y^a(x).
```

**Definition (proposal completeness).** A proposal/intervention family `A(x)` is
**(ε_C, ε_S, ε_B)-complete** for the shortcut `s` affecting `x` if there exists some
`a* ∈ A(x)` such that:

1. **Causal preservation.** `T_{a*}(x)` preserves the causal content up to *causal-channel
   damage* `ε_C`: the causal channel changes by at most `ε_C` per class.
2. **Shortcut neutralization.** `T_{a*}(x)` removes/neutralizes the shortcut
   contribution up to *residual shortcut* `ε_S`.
3. **No worse shortcut introduced.** `T_{a*}(x)` does not introduce a new shortcut
   larger than `ε_S` (the edit does not trade one spurious cue for a bigger one).
4. **Class-balanced residual.** Together (1)–(3) plus a small interaction residual
   `ε_ξ` imply the repaired logits stay close to the clean logits up to a
   *class-independent* offset:

   ```
   max_y | ρ_y^{a*}(x) - ρ̄^{a*}(x) | ≤ ε_B,    with   ε_B ≤ ε_C + ε_S + ε_ξ.
   ```

Conditions (1)–(3) are a *mechanistic* sufficient story; condition (4) is the
quantity the recovery theorem actually uses. We call any `a*` satisfying (4) a **good
(repairing) intervention** and any candidate violating it by a definite margin a
**harmful/non-repairing intervention**.

Completeness is an assumption about the *family*, not about CIC. It can hold or fail
independently of whether CIC's scorer can find `a*` — which is exactly why the next
section keeps the two apart.

## 3. Positive theorem: proposal-complete CIC repair

The theorem composes two independent facts: a **selection lemma** (CIC's ranking finds
a good intervention) and a **recovery lemma** (a good intervention repairs the
prediction — this is the per-input residual-to-clean corollary already proved in
`theory.md`).

**Lemma 1 (selection).** Suppose CIC scoring ranks at least one good intervention
`a* ∈ A(x)` above *all* harmful/non-repairing candidates by a margin `γ > 0`:
`score(a*) ≥ score(b) + γ` for every non-repairing `b`. If the estimation/scoring
noise is bounded by `γ/2` (each observed score deviates from its noiseless value by at
most `γ/2`), then the empirical top-1 candidate is a good intervention.

*Proof.* Noise of magnitude ≤ `γ/2` can change any pairwise score gap by at most `γ`.
Since `a*` leads every harmful candidate by more than `γ` noiselessly, its observed
score still exceeds theirs, so the empirical argmax is good (it is `a*` or another good
candidate that also outranks all harmful ones). ∎

**Lemma 2 (recovery — per-input residual-to-clean).** If `a` is a good intervention
(`max_y |ρ_y^a(x) - ρ̄^a(x)| ≤ ε_B`) and the clean causal margin satisfies
`m_clean(x) > 2 ε_B`, then `argmax_y ℓ_y(T_a(x)) = argmax_y ℓ_y(x_clean) = y*`.

*Proof.* Write `ℓ_y(T_a(x)) = ℓ_y(x_clean) + ρ̄^a(x) + r_y` with `|r_y| ≤ ε_B`. The
common offset `ρ̄^a(x)` shifts every class equally and cannot change the argmax. The
residual can shrink the gap between `y*` and any runner-up by at most
`|r_{y*}| + |r_y| ≤ 2 ε_B`; if `m_clean(x) > 2 ε_B` no runner-up overtakes `y*`. ∎
(This is the corollary in `theory.md` §9, restated.)

**Theorem 1 (proposal-complete CIC repair).** Suppose

* `A(x)` is `(ε_C, ε_S, ε_B)`-complete for the shortcut affecting `x` (Section 2);
* the clean causal margin satisfies `m_clean(x) > 2 ε_B`;
* CIC scoring ranks at least one good intervention above all harmful candidates by
  margin `γ` (Lemma 1 premise);
* scoring noise is below `γ/2`.

Then **top-1 CIC selects a good intervention** and the **repaired prediction equals the
clean/causal prediction**: `argmax_y ℓ_y(T_{a_top1}(x)) = y*`.

*Proof.* Lemma 1 gives that the top-1 candidate `a_top1` is good. Lemma 2 applied to
`a_top1` gives `argmax_y ℓ_y(T_{a_top1}(x)) = y*`. ∎

**Why this is the closest honest "global" theorem.** The guarantee references the
shortcut type *only* through proposal completeness and the margin condition. If those
hold, the conclusion is identical whether `s` is text, icon, watermark, background, or
texture. It is therefore **shortcut-type-agnostic under proposal completeness and a
class-balanced residual condition** — a general theorem *over intervention families*,
not over arbitrary unobserved shortcuts. It is **not** assumption-free; the
impossibility result (Section 6) shows it cannot be.

## 4. Top-k corollary

CIC can aggregate the top-`k` candidates (consensus neutralization) rather than commit
to top-1.

**Corollary 1 (top-k repair).** Let `R` be a repair operator that aggregates the
top-`k` CIC-ranked candidates into a single intervention `T_R`. Suppose:

* at least one good intervention `a*` appears in the top-`k` CIC-ranked candidates, and
* the aggregate intervention `T_R` does not exceed the good-intervention budget, i.e.
  it keeps the residual-to-clean class-balanced:
  `max_y |ρ_y^R(x) - ρ̄^R(x)| ≤ ε_B` (in particular causal damage stays ≤ `ε_C`).

Then under `m_clean(x) > 2 ε_B` the repaired prediction equals the clean prediction:
`argmax_y ℓ_y(T_R(x)) = y*`.

*Proof.* The second premise says `T_R` is itself a good intervention in the sense of
Section 2(4); apply Lemma 2 to `T_R`. ∎

**Honest caveat.** The second premise is a genuine assumption about the aggregator, not
a free consequence of "a good candidate is in the top-`k`". Two safe instantiations:

* **Select-best-by-stability** within the top-`k`: reduces to Theorem 1 with `k=1`
  effectively (the operator picks one candidate), so it inherits the top-1 guarantee
  whenever the selected one is good.
* **Averaging / consensus** over the top-`k`: the averaged residual is
  `ρ̄_avg = (1/k) Σ ρ^{a_i}`. Class-balance of the average is *not* implied by class-balance
  of one member; it requires the harmful members in the top-`k` to have residuals whose
  class-imbalance cancels or stays within `ε_B` after averaging. We do not claim this in
  general; it is an additional, checkable condition. Empirically CIC top-3 consensus has
  residual-to-clean comparable to top-1 (`theory.md`, Table; median ≈ 3.5 vs 3.7), i.e.
  the condition approximately holds on the text benchmark but is not guaranteed a priori.

## 5. Predictive success gate (observable certificate)

Theorem 1 uses `ε_B` and `m_clean`, which are *not directly observable at deployment*
(`ε_B` needs clean logits; `m_clean` needs `x_clean`). We give an observable certificate,
at the cost of one explicit empirical calibration step.

First, an observable-margin restatement of Lemma 2 that removes the dependence on
`m_clean`:

**Lemma 3 (observable-margin recovery).** If `max_y |ρ_y^a(x) - ρ̄^a(x)| ≤ ε_B` and the
**observed repaired margin** `m(T_a(x)) = ℓ_w(T_a(x)) - max_{y≠w} ℓ_y(T_a(x))` (with
`w = argmax_y ℓ_y(T_a(x))`) satisfies `m(T_a(x)) > 2 ε_B`, then
`argmax_y ℓ_y(T_a(x)) = y*`.

*Proof.* For any `y ≠ w`, `ℓ_w(x_clean) - ℓ_y(x_clean) = [ℓ_w(T_a(x)) - ℓ_y(T_a(x))] -
(r_w - r_y) ≥ m(T_a(x)) - 2 ε_B > 0`. So the repaired winner `w` also wins on the clean
logits, i.e. `w = y*`. ∎

The repaired margin `m(T_a(x))` *is* observable. The only unobservable quantity left is
`ε_B`. The gate bounds it from observables.

**Definition (success gate).** Let `G(x, a)` be a predicate over observable quantities:

* the CIC score gap between top-1 and the random/median candidate (and top-1/top-2
  separation),
* the repaired confidence margin `m(T_a(x))`,
* the stability gain after repair (instability before vs after neutralizing `a`),
* the selected region area not too large (bluntness bounded),
* a clean-safe / content-preservation score above threshold.

**Calibration step (explicit, empirical).** On a held-out validation set, fit a
threshold so that

```
G(x, a)  ⟹  max_y |ρ_y^a(x) - ρ̄^a(x)| ≤ ε̂      (with high probability).
```

This implication is **not** derivable a priori — it is an empirical bound `ε̂` learned
from validation data, where `ρ` *can* be measured because clean images are available
there. The gate is therefore a *calibrated* certificate, not a closed-form one.

**Theorem 2 (gated repair certificate).** If the selected intervention `a` passes the
gate `G(x, a)` (so `ε_B ≤ ε̂` with the calibrated confidence) and the observed repaired
margin satisfies `m(T_a(x)) > 2 ε̂`, then repair succeeds:
`argmax_y ℓ_y(T_a(x)) = y*` (with the calibrated confidence).

*Proof.* The gate gives `ε_B ≤ ε̂`; apply Lemma 3 with the bound `ε̂`. ∎

**Honesty.** Theorem 2 is only as strong as the calibration: the gate→`ε̂` step is an
empirical, validation-set claim with a confidence level, not a theorem. We state this
explicitly. The CIC clean-safe policy in the repository is exactly such a
validation-calibrated gate (prompt and neutralization strategy selected on validation
only), which is why repair is reported as a proof-of-concept with a measured clean-safe
drop rather than a closed-form guarantee.

## 6. Impossibility / no-free-lunch theorem

We now show the finite-candidate restriction is **fundamental**, not a weakness specific
to CIC.

**Setting.** A *finite-query black-box repair algorithm* `ALG` may query the classifier
on the input `x` and on `T_a(x)` for each `a` in a finite query set `Q ⊆ A(x)`,
observing only the returned logits. It must then output a repair action — apply some
intervention from a finite action set (in the natural CIC setting, the action set is
`A(x)` itself). `ALG` never observes the true label, `c`, `s`, or `x_clean`.

**Theorem 3 (no assumption-free universal repair).** For any finite-query black-box
algorithm `ALG`, there exist two data-generating worlds `W_1, W_2` (equivalently two
classifiers) that

* produce **identical observations** for `ALG` on `x` and on every queried
  intervention `T_a(x)`, `a ∈ Q`, yet
* admit **different correct repair actions**: the action `ALG` outputs (the same in both
  worlds, since its observations are identical) succeeds in one world and fails in the
  other.

Hence no finite-query black-box method can *guarantee* repair of an arbitrary shortcut.
Universal shortcut repair/discovery is impossible without proposal completeness,
structural assumptions, or access to an intervention family that contains a good
intervention.

*Proof sketch.* `ALG`'s output is a deterministic (or fixed-distribution) function of
its observation vector `O = (f(x), {f(T_a(x))}_{a ∈ Q})`. Construct the two worlds to
share exactly this `O`:

* In `W_1`, the shortcut affecting `x` is benign — `f` already predicts `y*` on `x`, and
  the best action is "do nothing" (or any neutral action). Define `f` on `x` and on all
  `T_a(x)`, `a ∈ Q`, to realize a chosen logit vector for each.
* In `W_2`, the shortcut flips the prediction, and the *only* intervention that restores
  `y*` is some `a* ∉ Q` (the true neutralizer is outside the query/proposal set — i.e.
  the family is **incomplete** for this shortcut). Choose `f` on `x` and on every
  `T_a(x)`, `a ∈ Q`, to equal the *same* logits as in `W_1`. This is possible because the
  classifier's behavior on the unqueried `a*` (and on `x_clean`) is unconstrained by `O`,
  so we are free to make the on-query behavior coincide while the off-query truth differs.

`ALG` sees identical `O` in both worlds, so it emits the same action `â`. In `W_1` any
neutral `â` is acceptable. In `W_2`, repair requires `a*`, which is either unavailable
(if the action set is `Q`/`A(x)` and `a* ∉ A(x)`) or indistinguishable from a harmful
candidate under `O`; so `â` fails to restore `y*` with certainty. Thus no single action
succeeds in both worlds, and `ALG` has no worst-case guarantee. ∎

**Interpretation.** The construction is exactly the failure of proposal completeness:
the bad world is the one where `A(x)` does **not** contain a good intervention, or where
two candidates are observationally identical under finite queries but only one repairs.
This is why "finite-candidate" is not a defect of CIC specifically — it is a necessary
condition for *any* counterfactual black-box shortcut method. Proposal completeness
(Section 2) is precisely the assumption that rules the bad world out.

## 7. Reframed contribution

**Old (weaker) framing.** "The theorem is text-overlay-specific and only directionally
supported."

**New (honest, stronger) framing.**

> The positive guarantee is **shortcut-type-agnostic under proposal completeness and
> a class-balanced residual condition** (Theorem 1). The negative theorem (Theorem 3)
> shows why **assumption-free open-world shortcut discovery is impossible** for finite
> black-box intervention methods. CIC therefore occupies a **principled middle ground**:
> it is not universal discovery, but it is a general intervention framework whenever the
> candidate proposals cover the shortcut. The theory is a **global theorem over
> intervention families, not over arbitrary unobserved shortcuts.**

This upgrades the *generality* of the positive statement (no longer "text only") without
upgrading the *strength* of the claim (still conditional, still finite-candidate, still
no open-world discovery).

## 8. Empirical connection

The theory is connected to the repository's existing experiments **without changing any
metric**. Proposal completeness and the selection margin are *approximately satisfied*
in the positive cases and *violated* in the negative case — which is what the theory
predicts.

| Experiment | Completeness (∃ good `a*`) | Selection (CIC ranks it) | Theory reading |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Text overlays** (headline) | approx. satisfied — text-region candidates cover the shortcut; per-input class-balance supported (`theory.md`) | yes — CIC top-1 beats matched random by ~0.42 | Theorem 1 premises approximately met |
| **Semantic-decoy icons** (2nd family) | approx. satisfied — decoy-region proposals cover the shortcut; passed 8/8 gates at n=64 and n=128 | yes — CIC top-1 0.711 vs random 0.258 | shortcut-type-agnostic positive case (icon, no text) |
| **Spatial-resolution audit** | approx. satisfied — selected regions are coarse (median IoU 0.39) but cover the shortcut (coverage ≥0.5 in 77%); low `ε_C` | yes — repair rises with coverage, 1.00 at IoU≥0.5 | low causal-channel damage `ε_C` is the operative variable, not exact IoU |
| **Watermark cross-shortcut** (negative) | **violated** — weak/flat channel, little shortcut to neutralize; `watermark_shortcut_channel_weak = true` | **violated** — frozen scorer cannot rank a good region | Theorem 1 premises fail ⇒ no repair, *consistent with* theory; near the impossibility regime |
| **Natural-text open-proposal** (next test) | to be tested — open candidate families (OCR + grid + connected components + high-contrast) probe whether completeness holds without a pre-specified shortcut family | to be tested | a direct empirical test of proposal completeness, still **not** open-world discovery (`open_world_claim_allowed = False`) |

The watermark case is the most informative: it is a measured instance of the
impossibility regime — when the family does not contain a good intervention (or the
shortcut channel is too weak for any candidate to be a good neutralizer), no finite
black-box method, CIC included, can repair, and the theory says so.

## 9. Wording discipline (overclaiming guard)

**Forbidden** (not used anywhere): "CIC solves open-world shortcut discovery";
"universal repair"; "fully general robustness"; "assumption-free guarantee"; "works for
all shortcuts".

**Allowed** (used in this document and the concise integrations): "shortcut-type-agnostic
under proposal completeness"; "general finite-intervention theorem"; "no-free-lunch
impossibility for assumption-free shortcut discovery"; "principled boundary between
open-world discovery and finite-candidate repair"; "global theorem over intervention
families, not over arbitrary unobserved shortcuts".

## 10. Validity summary

* **Positive theorem (Theorem 1): valid under its stated assumptions.** It composes a
  correct selection lemma with the already-proved per-input residual-to-clean recovery
  corollary. It is conditional, not assumption-free.
* **Top-k corollary (Corollary 1): valid**, with an explicit and necessary aggregator
  condition (averaging does not automatically inherit single-member class-balance).
* **Gate theorem (Theorem 2): valid given one explicit empirical calibration step**
  (the gate→`ε̂` implication is a validation-set bound, not a closed-form theorem). The
  observable-margin lemma (Lemma 3) is unconditionally valid.
* **Impossibility theorem (Theorem 3): valid** as a standard finite-query
  indistinguishability (no-free-lunch) argument; it justifies proposal completeness as a
  *necessary* assumption.

Net: there is no assumption-free universal theorem (Theorem 3 forbids it), and the
strongest honest positive statement is the proposal-complete, shortcut-type-agnostic
repair theorem (Theorem 1) with its observable, calibrated gate (Theorem 2).
