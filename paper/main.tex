\large A Finite-Candidate Intervention Framework for Detecting and Repairing

\author{Rohan Nagaram}
\date{\today}

\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Confidence measures how sure a model is, but not whether its certainty rests on
causal evidence. This paper proposes \emph{counterfactual stability} as a second
reliability axis and operationalizes it through \emph{Counterfactual Instability
Certificates} (CIC), a finite-candidate intervention framework for detecting and
repairing shortcut-dependent predictions. A \emph{shortcut} is a spurious feature
that correlates with the label but is not what actually defines it, and a model can
be confidently right for the wrong reason by leaning on one --- exactly the
high-confidence failure a confidence-only monitor misses. CIC scores, per example,
whether a prediction stays put when a hypothesized shortcut is changed while the
label-defining content is held fixed. It tests a \emph{finite, supplied candidate
set} of label-preserving interventions; it complements rather than replaces
confidence, and it does not search for unknown shortcuts.

Our primary benchmark is text-overlay shortcuts on real pretrained OpenCLIP
(\texttt{ViT-B-32}~/~\texttt{laion2b\_s34b\_b79k}; no fake backend for any
headline). On a frozen, held-out hard multi-decoy benchmark ($n=32$ per condition),
misleading text drops accuracy from near-perfect to $0.250$; non-oracle CIC region
scoring repairs it to $0.750$ versus $0.331$ for a matched random text-region
control, while the clean-safe accuracy drop is only $0.010$. The effect survives a
full benchmark-resampling audit and a failure-conditioned evaluation, and a
supporting scale/multi-model audit at $n=128$ reproduces it on \textbf{all four}
real pretrained OpenCLIP backbone/checkpoint pairs attempted (all repair-eligible;
CIC$-$random gaps $0.437$--$0.778$).

A second, non-text shortcut family shows the method is not text-specific:
\emph{semantic-decoy icons} (a central true-class icon plus a competing-class
corner icon, with \emph{no written words anywhere}) pass all eight strict gates at
both $n=64$ and $n=128$. At $n=128$, the decoy drops accuracy to $0.297$ while CIC
top-1 repair reaches $0.711$ versus $0.258$ matched random (gap $0.453$, clean-safe
drop $0.008$). A pooled spatial audit ($n=210$) fixes the method's resolution
honestly: exact localization remains limited (median IoU $0.39$ with the oracle
shortcut box), but selected regions intersect the shortcut in $90\%$ of cases and
cover at least half of it in $77\%$ --- so CIC is a \emph{coarse causal-intervention}
method, not a segmentation method.

The scope is deliberately bounded. CIC intervenes on and repairs a supplied finite
family of shortcut interventions; it makes \textbf{no} claim of open-world shortcut
discovery, exact bounding-box localization, general robustness, or
cross-shortcut transfer/universal repair (the frozen text policy does not transfer
to a weak-channel watermark shortcut). On theory, the strong \emph{global}
input-independent embedding-additivity assumption fails for OpenCLIP text overlays
--- the per-image shift is object-entangled --- but the weaker per-input
class-balance premise the recovery theorem actually needs is supported.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% =====================================================================
A deployed classifier that is wrong is a problem; a classifier that is
\emph{confidently} wrong, for a reason the operator cannot see, is a safety hazard.
Modern networks are routinely calibrated and gated on confidence, yet confidence
answers only one question: \emph{how sure is the model?} It is silent on a second,
equally important question: \emph{does that certainty depend on evidence that
actually determines the label, or on a shortcut?}

A \emph{shortcut} (or spurious feature) is predictive of the label in the training
distribution but is not causally necessary for the true class~\cite{geirhos2020shortcut}.
Vision--language models are a vivid example: pretrained CLIP can be flipped by a
small piece of overlaid text~\cite{goh2021multimodal,radford2021clip}, predicting the
written word rather than the depicted object --- often with high confidence. A
confidence-only monitor cannot catch this, because the failure is confident.

This report develops and evaluates a complementary signal. A
\textbf{Counterfactual Instability Certificate} (CIC) measures how much a
prediction moves when a hypothesized shortcut is changed while the label-defining
content is preserved. A reliable prediction should be \emph{stable} under such
label-preserving interventions; a shortcut-dependent prediction is \emph{unstable}.
We argue that confidence and counterfactual stability are two axes of a
\emph{reliability plane}, and that the most dangerous quadrant --- high confidence,
low stability --- is exactly the one confidence cannot police.

This work contributes a reliability framework, not merely a benchmark evaluation: it
defines counterfactual stability as an operational reliability axis, implements it as
a per-example intervention score, and tests whether finite-candidate shortcut
interventions can repair high-confidence shortcut failures in real pretrained
vision--language models.

\paragraph{Contributions and honest scope.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=2pt]
\item A reliability framework: counterfactual stability as a second axis beyond
confidence, with CIC as a per-example finite-candidate intervention score
(Sections~\ref{sec:cic}--\ref{sec:plane}).
\item An intervention-and-repair method for shortcut-dependent predictions in fixed
pretrained VLMs, evaluated on controlled text-overlay and non-text semantic-decoy
shortcut families. On a real-pretrained-OpenCLIP hard multi-decoy benchmark the
non-oracle finite-candidate method is corroborated by a full benchmark-resampling
audit, a failure-conditioned evaluation, and a second controlled shortcut family
(a non-text semantic-decoy icon) on which the same method passes all gates at $n=64$
and $n=128$ (Sections~\ref{sec:design}--\ref{sec:results}).
\item A conditional, finite-candidate recovery theory, gated on \emph{measured}
validation metrics: the strong global-additivity form fails for CLIP, but the
weaker per-input class-balance premise the theorem needs is supported
(Section~\ref{sec:theory}).
\item Retained negative results: no cross-shortcut transfer to watermarks; no exact
localization; no general robustness (Section~\ref{sec:negative}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background and Related Work}\label{sec:related}
% =====================================================================
CIC does not claim to invent shortcut learning, spurious correlations, or
counterfactual invariance; its contribution is an operational per-example reliability
certificate, a two-axis plane, and a finite-candidate counterfactual intervention and
repair framework. \emph{Shortcut learning} is a documented failure mode in which
models exploit background, texture, source, or annotation artifacts that correlate
with labels and fail under shift~\cite{geirhos2020shortcut}; benchmarks such as
Colored MNIST, Waterbirds, and CelebA separate a stable label feature from a
correlated nuisance, and overparameterization can worsen
reliance~\cite{sagawa2020investigation}. \emph{Robust-optimization} methods --- Group
DRO~\cite{sagawa2020groupdro} and IRM~\cite{arjovsky2019irm} --- target invariance
\emph{during training} and typically need group labels or multiple environments;
CIC is instead a diagnostic counterfactual-stability signal for a fixed pretrained
model and requires no retraining. \emph{Counterfactual invariance} and
counterfactually-augmented data~\cite{kaushik2020counterfactual,veitch2021counterfactual}
ask whether predictions stay stable under label-preserving edits; CIC uses that idea
at \emph{evaluation time} as a per-example certificate rather than a training
objective. CLIP and related VLMs are vulnerable to typographic / text-overlay
cues~\cite{radford2021clip,goh2021multimodal}, which we use as a controlled
finite-candidate shortcut benchmark on real pretrained
OpenCLIP~\cite{ilharco2021openclip,schuhmann2022laion5b}.

\paragraph{What CIC does differently.}
Closest to the repair setting are typographic-attack defenses that separate written
text from visual concepts in CLIP, e.g.\ representation-space procedures for
suppressing spelling-related directions~\cite{materzynska2022disentangling}. CIC
differs in three ways: it does \textbf{not} train or learn a global text-removal
direction, it does \textbf{not} modify the model, and it performs per-input,
finite-candidate counterfactual repair by ranking candidate regions by counterfactual
effect. This matters because our embedding-additivity validation finds typographic
shortcut shifts are object-entangled rather than a single global additive bias, so the
method targets the per-input region whose neutralization most improves stability.
Finally, selective classification / abstention~\cite{geifman2017selective} and
confidence/OOD baselines~\cite{hendrycks2017baseline} catch many failures, especially
low-confidence ones; CIC does not claim to dominate these everywhere --- its intended
advantage is on \emph{high-confidence} shortcut failures, and we report regimes where
confidence wins. Causal representation learning~\cite{scholkopf2021causal} motivates
treating label-defining structure as causal.

\section{Problem Setup}\label{sec:setup}
% =====================================================================
We adopt a structural view of data generation. A labelled input is
\begin{equation}
X = g(C, S, N), \qquad Y = h(C),
\end{equation}
where $C$ is the \emph{causal content} (the object/shape defining the label), $S$ is
a \emph{shortcut factor} (a spurious feature correlated with the label but not
label-defining --- e.g.\ a misleading text overlay or a colored-symbol watermark),
$N$ is \emph{nuisance} variation (placement jitter, decoys, background), and $h$
depends only on $C$. A classifier predicts $\argmax_y f_y(X)$ from per-class scores
(logits) $f_y(X)$.

The shortcut value ranges over a \textbf{finite candidate set}
$S \in \{s_0, s_1, \dots\}$ that includes a designated \emph{neutral value} $s_0$.
A \emph{counterfactual intervention} is a label-preserving change to $S$. This finite
candidate restriction is central and honest: CIC can only test the intervention
family it is given. When the true shortcut is absent from that family, CIC may
remain stable even though the model uses a different unstable feature.

% =====================================================================
\section{Counterfactual Instability Certificates}\label{sec:cic}
% =====================================================================
Given an input $x$ with prediction $\hat{y}(x) = \argmax_y f_y(x)$ and a set of
label-preserving counterfactuals $\{x^{(1)},\dots,x^{(K)}\}$ obtained by varying the
candidate shortcut, the \textbf{Counterfactual Instability Certificate} summarizes
how much the prediction (and its score distribution) moves across those
counterfactuals. Operationally we use prediction-flip rate and distributional shift
(Jensen--Shannon / KL) between $f(x)$ and $f(x^{(k)})$; high instability indicates
shortcut dependence.

\paragraph{CIC complements, not replaces, confidence.}
The repository's regime analysis makes the complementarity concrete. In a
\emph{confidence-solvable} regime, failures are already low-confidence and confidence
detects them (confidence AUROC $1.00$ vs.\ CIC $0.75$); CIC should not be promoted
there. In a \emph{confident-wrong} regime, where failures are high-confidence
shortcut failures, the ordering flips dramatically: CIC AUROC $\approx 0.9998$ while
confidence AUROC $\approx 0.28$ (worse than chance, because the wrong predictions are
\emph{more} confident). This is the precise sense in which CIC is a second axis.

% =====================================================================
\section{The Reliability Plane}\label{sec:plane}
% =====================================================================
Confidence measures \emph{how sure} a model is. Counterfactual stability
($1-\mathrm{CIC}$) measures \emph{whether that certainty depends on shortcut
evidence}. Plotting both axes yields four quadrants (Figure~\ref{fig:plane}):
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Reliable} --- high confidence, high stability.
\item \textbf{Uncertain but stable} --- low confidence, high stability (confidence
already flags these).
\item \textbf{Fragile} --- low confidence, low stability.
\item \textbf{Dangerous shortcut reliance} --- high confidence, low stability. This
is the quadrant confidence cannot police, and the target of this work.
\end{itemize}
In the repository's plane analysis, the dangerous quadrant contains $790$ examples
with a $41.5\%$ failure rate --- confident predictions that nonetheless rest on
unstable evidence.

\section{Experimental Design}\label{sec:design}
% =====================================================================
\paragraph{Model and backend.}
All headline evidence uses real pretrained OpenCLIP zero-shot classification
(\texttt{open\_clip}, \texttt{ViT-B-32}, \texttt{laion2b\_s34b\_b79k}). Every
headline artifact records \texttt{pretrained\_loaded = true} and
\texttt{fake\_backend = false}; results computed under a fallback/fake backend are
structurally blocked from headline eligibility.

\paragraph{Hard multi-decoy benchmark.}
Each image contains a rendered object plus multiple overlaid text regions. In the
\emph{misleading} condition, a harmful word (a competing class name) is overlaid
alongside neutral decoy words; \emph{aligned} overlays carry the true class word;
\emph{no-overlay} images carry the object alone. The benchmark is deliberately hard:
multiple decoys mean a naive ``read the biggest/most-text region'' heuristic is
insufficient.

\paragraph{Non-oracle scoring (leakage control).}
The non-oracle CIC scorer receives only image pixels, CLIP predictions, class
prompts, and a finite set of candidate region proposals. It never sees the true
label, the harmful overlay bounding box, the shortcut type, or per-example
correctness. True labels, oracle neutralization, and bounding boxes are used
\emph{only} to define held-out failure subsets, the oracle upper bound, and
localization metrics --- never inside the scorer.

\paragraph{Repair strategies and baselines.}
CIC region scoring ranks candidate regions by counterfactual instability and
neutralizes the top-$1$/top-$3$ regions (with a \emph{clean-safe} variant that
abstains from edits predicted to harm clean inputs). Controls include matched random
text-region repair (averaged over many random draws), random non-text patch repair,
random augmentation consensus, highest-textness, and largest-text repair. The
\emph{oracle} (known harmful box) repair is reported as an upper bound only.

\paragraph{Selective prediction.}
Adding CIC-guided abstention yields selective accuracy $0.929$ at coverage $0.984$
(2 abstentions, 35 repaired), i.e.\ near-perfect accuracy on covered examples.

\subsection{Full benchmark-resampling audit}
A single fixed benchmark cannot establish stability, so we ran a \emph{full
benchmark-resampling audit}: three independently resampled held-out instances, each
with a new object sequence, harmful class, decoy words, placements, image IDs, and
\emph{distinct image-set and metadata hashes}, under frozen generation and repair
policies. Across the three seeds, original misleading accuracy stayed low (mean
$0.260$, max $0.281$) and CIC top-1 repair held up (mean $0.750$, range
$0.719$--$0.813$) against matched random text repair (mean $0.289$), for a CIC$-$random
gap of mean $0.461$ (range $0.390$--$0.545$) at a clean drop of $\approx0.014$. All
three had original accuracy $\le 0.40$, all beat random by $\ge 0.15$, and
\texttt{full\_benchmark\_resampling\_stability\_supported = true}: the effect is stable
across resampled instances, not an artifact of one fixed draw.

\subsection{Second shortcut family: non-text semantic-decoy icon}
As positive supporting evidence---not a replacement for the text-overlay
headline---we re-ran the same finite-candidate CIC region method, from scratch
(not a frozen transfer), on an independent non-text shortcut family. Each stimulus
contains a central colored \emph{causal} icon (the true label) and a larger,
spatially separated, competing-class \emph{decoy} icon in a corner, with no written
words anywhere. The decoy's oracle box is used only for the oracle upper bound and a
diagnostic IoU; the non-oracle scorer sees only pixels, candidate boxes, and model
probabilities. On real pretrained OpenCLIP \texttt{ViT-B-32}~/~\texttt{laion2b\_s34b\_b79k}
(fake backend blocked), the decoy drives misleading-regime accuracy to $0.297$
while the central icon alone is perfectly recognized (clean $1.000$) and oracle
removal of the decoy fully restores it ($1.000$). The $n=64$ pilot passed all $8$
strict gates, and the $n=128$ scale run also passed all $8$ gates (no failed
gates), so the result is stable across scale.

\subsection{Failure-conditioned benchmark}
To probe repair precisely where the model fails, we built a failure-conditioned
subset. From $76$ generated candidates, $50$ verified failures were admitted
(inclusion rate $0.658$) under the predicate: pretrained CLIP classifies the
\emph{no-overlay} and \emph{aligned} images correctly, classifies the
\emph{misleading} image incorrectly with confidence $\ge 0.5$, \emph{and} oracle
harmful-text neutralization restores the correct prediction. By construction the
original failure-subset accuracy is $0$ --- this is \emph{not} a natural accuracy and
does not establish general robustness or discovery. We restate this caveat wherever
the number appears.

On these $50$ failures, CIC repairs $96$--$98\%$ (top-1 $0.960$, top-3 $0.980$,
clean-safe $0.940$), approaching the oracle upper bound ($1.000$), versus
$\approx 11.2\%\pm1.5\%$ for matched random text repair (gap $0.868$, non-overlapping
CIs); generic controls remain low (highest-textness $0.820$, largest-text $0.260$,
random augmentation consensus $0.020$, random non-text patch $0.002$), and clean and
aligned preservation after clean-safe repair are both $1.000$. Coarse localization is
$0.82$ (top-1) / $0.90$ (top-3) at IoU $\ge 0.3$ but only $0.04$ at IoU $\ge 0.5$;
repair is near-certain when coarse localization succeeds (IoU $\ge 0.3$: top-1 $=1.0$)
and \emph{still often succeeds} when it does not (IoU $<0.3$, $n=9$: top-1 $=0.78$). We
therefore claim \emph{coarse causal-region intervention}, not exact box localization.

\subsection{Spatial-resolution and causal-intervention audit}\label{sec:spatial}
Spatial precision remains a limitation, but the relevant intervention metric is not
exact box recovery alone. In a pooled spatial-resolution audit over $210$ shortcut
examples from the hard multi-decoy, failure-conditioned, and semantic-decoy
benchmarks, CIC-selected regions had median IoU $0.39$ with the oracle shortcut box
and reached IoU $\ge 0.5$ in $43\%$ of cases. However, they intersected the shortcut
in $90\%$ of cases and covered at least half of the shortcut box in $77\%$ of cases.
Selected regions were coarse, with median area $21\%$ of the image and about
$2.1\times$ the oracle shortcut box area. Repair accuracy rose sharply with coarse
overlap: CIC top-1 repair was $0.92$ for $0.3 \le \text{IoU} < 0.5$ and $1.00$ for
IoU $\ge 0.5$, while remaining low below IoU $0.3$. Thus CIC should be understood as a
coarse causal-intervention method rather than an exact localization or segmentation
method. The audit reads existing artifacts only and changes no headline metric; a
non-oracle refinement diagnostic (geometric variants re-scored using only pixels,
candidate boxes, and model probabilities) did \emph{not} improve median IoU or the
IoU $\ge 0.5$ rate, reported honestly as such. Repair accuracy rises sharply across
exact-IoU buckets (top-1 repair $0.264$ at IoU $<0.1$, $0.919$ at $0.3\le$IoU$<0.5$,
$1.000$ at IoU $\ge0.5$). Source:
\texttt{results/spatial\_resolution\_audit/}.

\subsection{COCO-Text natural-image validation}\label{sec:cocotext}
The controlled benchmarks above use generated stimuli. To test CIC on real
photographs with dataset-provided annotations, we evaluated it on COCO-Text, where
many natural images contain incidental scene text that can act as a shortcut. We
used the same shortcut-agnostic, proposal-based CIC region method (no pre-specified
shortcut family), scoring open region proposals on real pretrained OpenCLIP
\texttt{ViT-B-32}~/~\texttt{laion2b\_s34b\_b79k} (fake backend blocked) over all
$500$ COCO-Text metadata rows. From these we isolated $57$ directionally verified
text-driven failures and a stricter subset of $39$ oracle-repairable failures.

\emph{On COCO-Text, CIC provides dataset-backed directional repair evidence rather
than localization support.} On the strict oracle-repairable subset, proposal-based
CIC improves alias-aware repair from $0.205$ (matched random) to $0.538$ (gap
$+0.333$), raises the target-probability improvement rate to $0.69$ (vs.\ $0.49$),
decreases the text-distractor probability in $0.95$ of cases, and recovers the correct
label in oracle top-3/top-5 of $0.90$/$1.00$; the directional subset shows the same
pattern ($0.439$ vs.\ $0.175$, gap $+0.263$). Over all $500$ rows the gap is $-0.040$,
as expected since most examples are already correct and need no repair, and reading OCR
text into the candidate set helps only marginally
(\texttt{ocr\_inclusion\_materially\_helps = false}). Crucially, selected proposals
overlap annotated COCO-Text boxes in only $0.128$ of strict cases, so the strict and
directional support gates remain \texttt{false} (overlap $0.128$/$0.140 < 0.60$ gate).
A localization diagnostic attributes this to ranking, not coverage: text-overlapping
proposals exist in over half of strict cases but are ranked below more causally
effective non-text regions (ranking accounts for ${\sim}92\%$ of the shortfall), and
\emph{forcing} the best text-overlapping proposal repairs \emph{worse} than the
selected CIC region. Thus COCO-Text supports CIC as a causal repair signal on natural
images while exposing a boundary: repair does not imply text-box localization. This is
\textbf{not} open-world shortcut discovery and does \textbf{not} claim CIC localizes
scene text. Per-subset and diagnostic metrics:
\texttt{results/coco\_text\_cic\_full/} and the artifact index.

\subsection{Predictive CIC abstention gate}\label{sec:predgate}
The results above ask whether CIC repair \emph{works}; deployment also needs to know
\emph{when to trust an individual repair before the true label is available}. We add a
\textbf{label-free predictive abstention layer}: from inference-time observables only
(repaired confidence/margin, top-$k$ repair agreement, entropy drop, stability gain,
prediction-changed flag --- no true label, correctness, oracle outcome, or box), a
logistic gate decides \textbf{accept} or \textbf{abstain}. Calibrated over $2{,}635$
examples from $8$ benchmarks (repair-success base rate $0.785$), under
leave-one-benchmark-out it reaches \textbf{AUROC $0.789$}, and under abstention the
accepted repairs are high-precision (top-$25\%$ accepted precision $0.97$; top-$10\%$
$0.99$). The best interpretable rule is simply \emph{accept iff CIC did not change the
prediction}. The gate is strongest in pooled/controlled settings and \textbf{does not
transfer reliably to the hardest COCO-Text subsets} (held-out AUROC $0.347$ strict /
$0.296$ directional; controlled$\to$natural $\approx0.50$), so
\texttt{predictive\_gate\_supported = true} while \texttt{is\_universal\_theorem =
false}. Source: \texttt{results/predictive\_cic\_gate/}.

This gate is the calibrated, observable instance of the \textbf{predictive CIC
certificate} (Proposition~\ref{prop:predcert}, Section~\ref{sec:predcert};
machine-checked in
\texttt{causal\_reliability/theory/predictive\_certificate.py}): if the repaired
prediction margin exceeds an \emph{empirically calibrated residual-instability bound}
$\hat\epsilon$ ($m_{\mathrm{rep}}(x) > 2\hat\epsilon$), the repaired prediction is
stable under the calibrated perturbation class, giving a \textbf{label-free
accept/abstain rule}. It substitutes a calibrated bound for the residual budget
$\epsilon_B$ of the per-input class-balance recovery lemma
(Section~\ref{sec:theory}) and reads the margin off the observed repaired logits, so
it requires \textbf{no true labels at inference time}. This is a
\emph{conditional predictive certificate} and an abstention layer, not a universal
correctness oracle.

\section{Theory and Mechanism}\label{sec:theory}
% =====================================================================
We state a conditional, finite-candidate recovery theory and report whether its
assumptions hold for OpenCLIP. The theory explains \emph{when} neutralizing a
shortcut region restores the causal prediction; it does not prove discovery, exact
localization, or robustness.

\subsection{Additive-channel assumption}
We assume the per-class logit decomposes into a causal channel, a shortcut channel,
and a small interaction residual:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:additive}
\ell_y\big(g(C,S,N)\big) = \phi_y(C,N) + \psi_y(S) + \xi_y(C,S,N).
\end{equation}
For zero-shot CLIP, logits are inner products $\ell_y(X)=\langle u(X), v_y\rangle$,
so \eqref{eq:additive} is additive in the embedding exactly when the embedding is
additive: the shortcut channel $\psi_y(S)=\langle \delta_S, v_y\rangle$ has a
well-defined, approximately \emph{input-independent} direction $\delta_S$ iff
$u(\text{object}+\text{shortcut}) - u(\text{object}) \approx \delta_S$ independent of
the object. This reduces the assumption to a measurable claim about embedding
geometry.

\subsection{Exact and approximate recovery}
\begin{theorem}[Exact neutralization]
If \eqref{eq:additive} holds with $\xi_y\equiv 0$ and the neutral value $s_0$ makes
the shortcut channel class-independent ($\psi_y(s_0)=c$ for all $y$), then
$\argmax_y[\phi_y(C,N)+\psi_y(s_0)] = \argmax_y \phi_y(C,N)$. Neutralization recovers
the causal argmax.
\end{theorem}
A class-independent offset cannot change which class wins. Allowing residual error,
let the causal margin be $m(X)=\phi_{y^*}(C,N)-\max_{y\ne y^*}\phi_y(C,N)$ and bound
the causal-channel damage, residual shortcut, and interaction by
$\epsilon_C,\epsilon_S,\epsilon_\xi$.
\begin{theorem}[Approximate recovery]
If $m(X) > 2(\epsilon_C+\epsilon_S+\epsilon_\xi)$, then the post-neutralization
argmax equals the causal argmax $y^*$.
\end{theorem}
Each error term moves a score by at most its bound; the worst-case winner/runner-up
swing is twice the summed bounds.

\subsection{Class-balanced sweep corollary}
Averaging logits over a class-balanced sweep $B$ of shortcut values,
$\bar f_y(X)=\frac{1}{|B|}\sum_{s\in B}\ell_y(g(C,s,N))$, cancels the shortcut
contribution up to a class-independent offset whenever the average shortcut channel
$\frac{1}{|B|}\sum_{s\in B}\psi_y(s)$ is class-independent --- the basis for
consensus/sweep repair.

\subsection{Object-entanglement: the global form fails for CLIP}
The embedding-additivity validation tests the \emph{strong, global} form of
\eqref{eq:additive} on real OpenCLIP. The result is a negative-but-informative
structural finding (Table~\ref{tab:theory}, Figure~\ref{fig:entangle}):
\begin{quote}
\itshape OpenCLIP's typographic shortcut effect is not a single global additive bias
direction. The shift induced by overlay text is \textbf{object-entangled}: it
contains a real shortcut component, but its direction varies substantially with the
underlying object.
\end{quote}
Concretely, same-shortcut deltas cluster above the shuffled-label baseline
(within-shortcut cosine $0.765$ vs.\ shuffled $0.634$), confirming a real shortcut
component, and the logit decomposition is exactly additive in embedding space
(consistency MAE $\approx 6\times10^{-15}$). \emph{But} the per-image delta clusters
\emph{more} tightly by object class than by shortcut value (within-object cosine
$0.855 > 0.765$), so $\delta_S$ is not input-independent, and the neutralization
damage is not small relative to the multi-decoy shortcut effect (ratio $0.92$).
Hence \texttt{embedding\_additivity\_supported\_for\_text = false}: the global
additivity theorem is \textbf{not} claimed to apply to CLIP. This explains why
generic global debiasing is unlikely to suffice, while targeted per-input region
scoring still repairs failures.

\subsection{Per-input residual-to-clean class-balance: the weaker premise the theorem needs}
Global additivity is stronger than recovery requires, and the relevant baseline is
the \emph{clean} prediction, not the misleading input. Let $x_{\mathrm{clean}}$ be
the no-overlay/clean image with the same causal content, and let $T(x)$ be the
neutralized/repaired version of the misleading image. Define the per-class
\textbf{residual-to-clean}
\begin{equation}
\rho_y(x) = \ell_y\big(T(x)\big) - \ell_y\big(x_{\mathrm{clean}}\big),
\qquad
\bar\rho(x) = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{Y}|}\sum_{y\in\mathcal{Y}}\rho_y(x).
\end{equation}
The \textbf{per-input residual-to-clean class-balance condition} is
\begin{equation}
\max_{y\in\mathcal{Y}} \big|\rho_y(x)-\bar\rho(x)\big| \le \epsilon_B,
\end{equation}
which allows the shortcut effect to vary across inputs as long as the repaired
logits differ from the clean logits by an approximately class-independent residual
\emph{within} each input. We define the residual relative to the clean/causal
logits, not relative to the misleading input logits: a shift that is class-balanced
relative to the misleading logits would preserve the misleading argmax, whereas
recovery requires returning to the clean causal argmax.

\begin{corollary}[Per-input residual-to-clean recovery]
If the repaired logits differ from the clean logits by an approximately
class-independent residual ($\max_y|\rho_y(x)-\bar\rho(x)|\le\epsilon_B$) and the
clean causal margin satisfies $m_{\mathrm{clean}}(x)>2\epsilon_B$, then
\begin{equation}
\argmax_y \ell_y\big(T(x)\big) = \argmax_y \ell_y\big(x_{\mathrm{clean}}\big).
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Write $\ell_y(T(x))=\ell_y(x_{\mathrm{clean}})+\bar\rho(x)+r_y(x)$ with
$|r_y(x)|\le\epsilon_B$. The common offset $\bar\rho(x)$ shifts all classes equally
and cannot change the argmax; the residual can shrink the clean winner's margin by at
most $2\epsilon_B$. Hence if $m_{\mathrm{clean}}(x)>2\epsilon_B$, the clean winner
remains the repaired winner.
\end{proof}

The earlier global additivity test asked whether overlay-induced embedding shifts
were input-independent across images. The residual-to-clean test is weaker and more
directly tied to recovery: it asks whether a particular neutralization leaves the
repaired logits close to the clean logits up to an approximately class-independent
offset. This is the quantity reported in Table~\ref{tab:theory}, measured in
residual-to-clean logit units.

This weaker premise \emph{is} supported on OpenCLIP text overlays
(\texttt{per\_input\_class\_balance\_supported\_for\_text = true},
\texttt{clip\_theory\_support\_status = ``CLIP-supported via per-input
class-balance''}). With $\epsilon_B=3.0$ logits, oracle and CIC neutralization are
substantially more class-balanced (lower residual-to-clean) than matched random
text-region neutralization, and the margin condition tracks repair success: when
satisfied, repair succeeds $100\%$ of the time across all conditions
(Table~\ref{tab:theory}, Figure~\ref{fig:classbal}). The mechanism is validated most
tightly for oracle neutralization and approximately for CIC. Because CIC's median
residual-to-clean ($\approx 3.7$ top-1, $\approx 3.5$ top-3) exceeds
$\epsilon_B=3.0$, the theorem does not fully explain every CIC success: CIC
neutralization is more class-balanced than matched random repair and aligns with the
recovery condition directionally, but the worst-case margin condition is
conservative, and many successful CIC repairs occur even when the sufficient
condition is not formally satisfied.

\paragraph{Repair advantage over matched random.}
Heuristically, the expected gap between CIC and a matched random control,
$\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{CIC}]-\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{random}]\approx
\big(q-\tfrac{r}{m}\big)(p_u-p_d)$, increases with the probability $q$ that CIC ranks
the true shortcut region highly relative to the fraction $r/m$ of useful regions a
random draw hits, scaled by the difference between up-repair and down-repair
probabilities. The measured gaps ($0.42$ main, $0.46$ resampled, $0.87$
failure-conditioned) are consistent with CIC's region ranking being substantially
better than chance.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
The theorem's variable is \emph{low causal-channel damage}, not exact IoU: a coarse
region that covers the shortcut while leaving the object intact yields small
$\epsilon_C$. This is why repair succeeds at coarse IoU $\ge 0.3$ while exact IoU
$\ge 0.5$ stays weak. The account is conditional and finite-candidate only.

\subsection{Shortcut-type-agnostic generalization and a no-free-lunch boundary}
\label{sec:global}
The recovery results above are stated for a shortcut region/value but generalize to
\emph{arbitrary} shortcut types under one structural assumption on the candidate set,
\emph{without} strengthening the claim. The full development is in
\texttt{docs/global\_cic\_theory\_attempt.md} with a machine-checked encoding in
\texttt{causal\_reliability/theory/}; we summarize it here. Index the interventions by
candidate $a$ in a proposal family $\mathcal{A}(x)$, with residual-to-clean
$\rho^a_y(x)=\ell_y(T_a(x))-\ell_y(x_{\mathrm{clean}})$.

\begin{definition}[Proposal completeness]
$\mathcal{A}(x)$ is $(\epsilon_C,\epsilon_S,\epsilon_B)$-complete for shortcut $s$ if
some $a^\star\in\mathcal{A}(x)$ preserves the causal content up to damage $\epsilon_C$,
neutralizes the shortcut up to residual $\epsilon_S$, introduces no larger new
shortcut, and leaves a class-balanced residual
$\max_y|\rho^{a^\star}_y(x)-\bar\rho^{a^\star}(x)|\le\epsilon_B$ (with
$\epsilon_B\le\epsilon_C+\epsilon_S+\epsilon_\xi$). Such an $a^\star$ is a
\emph{good intervention}.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Proposal-complete CIC repair]
If $\mathcal{A}(x)$ is complete for $s$, $m_{\mathrm{clean}}(x)>2\epsilon_B$, CIC
scoring ranks a good intervention above all harmful candidates by margin $\gamma$, and
scoring noise is below $\gamma/2$, then top-1 CIC selects a good intervention and the
repaired prediction equals the clean causal prediction $y^\star$. The conclusion is
\textbf{independent of whether the shortcut is text, icon, watermark, background, or
texture}, as long as the family contains a good intervention.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
A selection lemma (noise $<\gamma/2$ cannot overturn a $\gamma$-margin ranking, so the
empirical top-1 is good) composed with the per-input residual-to-clean recovery
corollary applied to the selected intervention.
\end{proof}
A top-$k$ consensus corollary holds when the aggregated intervention keeps the residual
class-balanced (averaging does not inherit single-member class-balance for free). Since
$\epsilon_B$ and $m_{\mathrm{clean}}$ are not observable at deployment, an
observable-margin lemma certifies recovery from the \emph{repaired} margin
($m(T_a(x))>2\epsilon_B \Rightarrow$ repaired argmax $=$ clean argmax), and a
\emph{validation-calibrated} gate over observables (score gap, repaired margin,
stability gain, selected area, clean-safe score) upper-bounds $\epsilon_B\le\hat\epsilon$
so repair is certified when the gate holds and $m(T_a(x))>2\hat\epsilon$; the
gate$\Rightarrow\hat\epsilon$ step is an explicit empirical calibration, not a
closed-form bound.

\begin{theorem}[No assumption-free universal repair]
For any finite-query black-box repair algorithm there exist two worlds that produce
identical observations on every queried intervention but have different unobservable
causal labels $y^\star$; the algorithm's single action repairs in one world and fails in
the other. Hence no finite-query black-box counterfactual method can \emph{guarantee}
discovery or repair of an arbitrary shortcut without proposal completeness or equivalent
structural access to a good intervention.
\end{theorem}
This shows the finite-candidate restriction is a \emph{necessary} condition for any
counterfactual shortcut method, not a weakness of CIC specifically: it is exactly the
failure of proposal completeness made concrete. The weak/flat watermark channel
(Section~\ref{sec:negative}) is a measured instance of this regime. We therefore frame
the positive guarantee as \textbf{shortcut-type-agnostic under proposal completeness and
a class-balanced residual condition} and the negative result as a \textbf{no-free-lunch
impossibility for assumption-free shortcut discovery}: CIC is a general theorem over
intervention families, not over arbitrary unobserved shortcuts --- a principled boundary
between open-world discovery and finite-candidate repair, not universal discovery and not
universal repair.

\subsection{Complete finite-candidate characterization of CIC repair}
\label{sec:finitechar}
The results above are \emph{sufficient} conditions. We now close the finite-candidate
setting with a \textbf{complete characterization}: an exact criterion, a \emph{tight}
margin certificate, a coverage ceiling, and a repair--localization conflict. This is a
characterization \emph{inside a finite candidate set} --- \textbf{not} open-world
discovery, semantic correctness, exact localization, or general robustness. Index
interventions by candidate $a\in\mathcal{A}(x)$ with repaired logits $z_a=f(T_a(x))$,
clean logits $z_{\mathrm{clean}}$, residual $r_a=z_a-z_{\mathrm{clean}}$, and causal
label $y^\star$ (used for evaluation only). Full proofs:
\texttt{docs/theory.md}~\S11; machine-checked in
\texttt{causal\_reliability/theory/finite\_candidate\_characterization.py}
(\texttt{tests/test\_finite\_candidate\_characterization.py}).

\begin{theorem}[Exact repair criterion]
Candidate $a$ repairs iff $z_a(y^\star) > \max_{j\neq y^\star} z_a(j)$; equivalently,
with $z_a=z_{\mathrm{clean}}+r_a$, iff $z_{\mathrm{clean}}(y^\star)-z_{\mathrm{clean}}(j)
> r_a(j)-r_a(y^\star)$ for every $j\neq y^\star$. This is necessary and sufficient.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Tight residual-margin certificate]
Let $m_{\mathrm{clean}}=z_{\mathrm{clean}}(y^\star)-\max_{j\neq y^\star}z_{\mathrm{clean}}(j)$
and bound the residual imbalance by $\max_y|r_a(y)-\bar r_a|\le\epsilon$. \emph{(Sufficiency)}
If $m_{\mathrm{clean}}>2\epsilon$ then $a$ repairs. \emph{(Tightness)} If
$m_{\mathrm{clean}}\le 2\epsilon$, the residual $r_a(y^\star)=-\epsilon$, $r_a(j)=+\epsilon$
on the leading competitor (imbalance exactly $\epsilon$) flips the top class. The
threshold $2\epsilon$ is therefore sharp.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Proposal coverage ceiling]
Define $R_\tau(x)=\mathbf{1}[\exists\,a\in\mathcal{A}(x):\mathrm{overlap}(a)\ge\tau]$.
For \emph{any} scoring/ranking method choosing one candidate per input, localization
success at threshold $\tau$ obeys
$\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{1}[\mathrm{overlap}(\mathrm{selected})\ge\tau]]\le\mathbb{E}[R_\tau(x)]$,
with equality for the oracle highest-overlap scorer.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Repair--localization conflict]
There exist finite-candidate repair problems where the repair-optimal proposal is not
the localization-optimal proposal: e.g.\ a text-overlapping proposal with high human-box
overlap but weak repair effect coexists with a non-text region of low text overlap but
stronger repair effect. Hence repair success does not imply text-box localization.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}[Proof sketches]
Theorem~1 is the definition of repair (strict argmax $=y^\star$) rearranged through
$z_a=z_{\mathrm{clean}}+r_a$. Theorem~2 bounds the worst-case pairwise swing
$r_a(j)-r_a(y^\star)\le 2\epsilon$ and exhibits a mean-zero witness attaining it.
Theorem~3 is the pointwise inequality $\mathbf{1}[\mathrm{overlap}(\mathrm{selected})\ge\tau]
\le R_\tau(x)$ under expectation. Theorem~4 is an explicit two-candidate construction.
Full details in \texttt{docs/theory.md}~\S11.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{This explains COCO-Text.} The characterization makes the natural-image
limitation (Section~\ref{sec:cocotext}) \emph{theoretically expected}. On the strict
oracle-repairable subset, CIC beats matched random repair by $+0.333$ (Theorems~1--2:
the selected intervention restores the causal margin), yet selected proposals overlap
annotated text boxes in only $0.128$ of cases. The localization diagnostic shows
text-overlapping proposals \emph{exist} ($0.564$ of strict cases) but are ranked below
more causally effective regions (ranking $\approx 92\%$ of the shortfall, coverage
ceilings the rest --- Theorem~3), and \emph{forcing} the best text-overlap proposal
repairs \emph{worse} ($0.163$ vs.\ selected $0.439$ --- Theorem~4). The natural-image
localization gap is thus a predicted consequence of the finite-candidate
characterization, not merely an implementation failure; COCO-Text is reported as
\textbf{directional repair evidence}, not localization support.

\subsection{Predictive CIC abstention certificate}
\label{sec:predcert}
The positive theorems above are stated in unobservable quantities (the clean causal
margin $m_{\mathrm{clean}}$ and residual budget $\epsilon_B$). For deployment we want
a \emph{label-free} rule that decides, after CIC has already selected a repair from its
finite proposal set, whether to trust that repair. We record a conditional certificate
in purely observable, inference-time quantities; it is the formal object that the
predictive gate of Section~\ref{sec:predgate} operationalizes. The machine-checkable
encoding is \texttt{causal\_reliability/theory/predictive\_certificate.py} (tested in
\texttt{tests/test\_predictive\_cic\_gate.py}).

For the repaired input $T_a(x)$ selected by CIC, let $\hat y=\argmax_y f_y(T_a(x))$ and
define the \textbf{repaired margin}
$m_{\mathrm{rep}}(x) = f_{\hat y}(T_a(x)) - \max_{y\neq\hat y} f_y(T_a(x))$. Assume every
perturbation in a calibrated perturbation class $\mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{cal}}$ changes any
single class score by at most $\hat\epsilon$, where $\hat\epsilon$ is an
\emph{empirically calibrated} upper bound (a margin quantile over validation
residual-to-clean class imbalances) substituted for the unobservable budget $\epsilon_B$.

\begin{proposition}[Margin-based predictive repair certificate]\label{prop:predcert}
If $m_{\mathrm{rep}}(x) > 2\hat\epsilon$, then the repaired prediction $\hat y$ is
invariant to all residual perturbations in $\mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{cal}}$.
\end{proposition}
\emph{Proof sketch.} A perturbation lowers the top score and raises a competitor by at
most $\hat\epsilon$ each, so the margin shrinks by at most $2\hat\epsilon$; if it started
above $2\hat\epsilon$ it stays positive and the argmax is unchanged, else the rule
abstains (full proof in \texttt{docs/theory.md}).

\paragraph{Interpretation.} The certificate is deliberately narrow: it does \textbf{not}
prove semantic correctness or that CIC found the true shortcut. It gives a
\emph{label-free accept/abstain rule} for an already-proposed CIC repair --- accept when
the repaired margin exceeds the calibrated instability bound, abstain otherwise --- and is
the formal object the empirical gate of Section~\ref{sec:predgate} operationalizes (which
generalizes the single margin test to a few observable features, reaching pooled LOBO
AUROC $0.789$ but remaining weak on hard COCO-Text, hence \textbf{not} a universal
correctness oracle). It thus occupies the middle ground between the positive conditional
theorem (\emph{when} a complete proposal set repairs) and the impossibility theorem
(\emph{why} completeness cannot be guaranteed for arbitrary unknown shortcuts).

\section{Negative Results and Boundary Conditions}\label{sec:negative}
% =====================================================================
We retain negative results rather than hide them.

\paragraph{Cross-shortcut transfer fails (watermarks).}
We froze the text-overlay-selected CIC policy and applied it, \emph{with no
retuning}, to a non-text colored-symbol watermark shortcut on real OpenCLIP. In the
natural benchmark, CLIP barely reads the watermark (original misleading accuracy
$0.75$, i.e.\ the shortcut is weak), and in the failure-conditioned mode only $4$ of
$384$ candidates ($1.0\%$) met the failure predicate. The frozen CIC repair reached
only $0.50$ on that tiny subset; \texttt{cross\_shortcut\_headline\_eligible =
false} (failure reasons: too few failures and natural accuracy not low enough;
frozen CIC top-1/3 $<0.70$; clean preservation not high). \textbf{We claim no
cross-shortcut generalization.} The main claim remains text-region finite-candidate
repair. The embedding-additivity validation explains this: the watermark shortcut
channel is weak/flat (within-object cosine $0.923 \gg$ within-shortcut $0.757$,
\texttt{watermark\_shortcut\_channel\_weak = true}), so there is little shortcut
contribution to neutralize --- a transfer failure \emph{consistent with} the theory
rather than a refutation. (Sources:
\texttt{results/cross\_shortcut\_generalization/}, \texttt{results/embedding\_additivity/}.)

\paragraph{No exact localization; no general robustness.}
Localization is strong at coarse IoU $\ge 0.3$ and weak at IoU $\ge 0.5$ across all
settings, so we claim coarse causal-region localization only. The benchmark is
controlled and generated; we make no broad real-world robustness claim.

\section{Codebase and Reproducibility Audit}\label{sec:repro}
% =====================================================================
\paragraph{Controls, leakage prevention, and tests.}
Experiments are config-driven and every run writes structured artifacts under
\texttt{results/}; the full repository/file inventory is in
\texttt{FINAL\_ARTIFACT\_INDEX.md}. Reproducibility is enforced by separating a
fixed-benchmark determinism check from true resampling (the resampling audit records
distinct \texttt{image\_set\_hash}/\texttt{metadata\_hash} per seed; identical hashes
would invalidate a stability claim). The non-oracle scorer is structurally denied the
true label, harmful bbox, shortcut type, and correctness --- these are used only to
define failure subsets, oracle bounds, and localization metrics --- and a fallback/fake
backend is blocked from headline eligibility (\texttt{headline\_eligible} is gated on
pretrained loading, leakage checks, low original accuracy, beating matched random, and
clean preservation). Paper numbers come from regenerated artifacts (chiefly
\texttt{final\_key\_numbers.json}); the test suite passes \textbf{382/382}
(\texttt{python3 -m pytest}) with tests for no-oracle-leakage, generalization/theory
gating, class-balance, and final-report caveats, and $24/24$ negative controls pass. An
earlier exploratory headline ($0.219\to0.875$) under a prior prediction path is
\emph{not} used; the authoritative artifacts report $0.250\to0.750$ vs.\ matched random
$0.331$, and the artifact index marks the stale value as historical.

\paragraph{Human label-preservation validation.}
Three annotators evaluated $100$ original/repaired image pairs ($300$ annotations).
Majority vote found the object label preserved in $96/100$ pairs and the repaired image
recognizable in $97/100$, with high inter-annotator agreement (Fleiss' $\kappa$
$0.973$/$0.974$/$0.920$/$1.000$ for before-label, after-label, label-change, and
recognizability). The four preservation failures were retained and flagged rather than
removed, and before/after accuracy against the true label is reported only when
pair-level labels are present (otherwise n/a; no labels were fabricated). The study
covers text-overlay repair pairs; broader validation across families remains future
work.

\paragraph{WILDS Waterbirds diagnostic and honest limitations.}
WILDS Waterbirds was parsed as a real spurious-background diagnostic: metadata-only
OpenCLIP showed the expected background sensitivity (overall $56.0\%$; land $73.1\%$
vs.\ water $35.9\%$; landbird $74.4\%$ on land vs.\ $21.6\%$ on water), but it ships no
oracle-repairable masks, so CIC repair was not run --- it is motivation/future work, not
a positive result. The resampling audit used fewer random-baseline repeats per seed to
bound runtime (wider per-seed CIs than the $100$-seed main run), and the
failure-conditioned and cross-shortcut evaluations are small-$n$ by construction.

\section{Limitations}\label{sec:limits}
% =====================================================================
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Finite candidate class only.} CIC tests the intervention family it is
given; it is \textbf{not} open-world shortcut discovery.
\item \textbf{No exact localization.} Coarse causal-region localization (IoU $\ge0.3$)
only; IoU $\ge0.5$ is weak (pooled median IoU $0.39$). CIC is a coarse
causal-intervention method, not segmentation.
\item \textbf{Not a text detector.} The most causally effective intervention need not
be the literal annotated text box: on COCO-Text, repair improves
($0.205\to0.538$) without text-box localization (overlap $0.128$, ranking-dominated),
so this is directional repair evidence, not scene-text detection.
\item \textbf{No general robustness / no cross-shortcut transfer.} Results are on
controlled, generated benchmarks (plus COCO-Text); the text-region policy did not
transfer to the watermark shortcut.
\item \textbf{Failure-conditioned conditioning.} The $96$--$98\%$ repair is on a subset
where CLIP already fails (original accuracy $0$ by construction), not a natural accuracy.
\item \textbf{Predictive gate is an abstention layer, not an oracle.} Controlled$\to$
natural transfer is weak on hard COCO-Text (held-out AUROC $0.347$ strict / $0.296$
directional).
\item \textbf{Theory caveat.} Global input-independent additivity failed; the theory is
supported only via the weaker per-input class-balance premise.
\item \textbf{Human validation / Waterbirds scope.} Human validation covers $100$
text-overlay pairs with three annotators; WILDS Waterbirds is a metadata-only
spurious-background diagnostic (no oracle masks, so no CIC repair). Both are scoped
honestly as future work beyond their current setting.
\end{itemize}

\section{Frozen generalization map}\label{sec:frozenmap}
% =====================================================================
The final study is intentionally framed as a finite-candidate reliability framework.
The table separates supported claims from explicit non-claims so that controlled
repair, natural-image directional evidence, localization limits, predictive
abstention, and theory are not conflated.

CIC is \emph{frozen} as a finite-candidate counterfactual intervention framework:
the method tests a hypothesized or proposed family of label-preserving interventions,
not arbitrary unobserved shortcuts. Different experiments test different pieces of the
same claim rather than one monolithic benchmark. Controlled benchmarks
(Sections~\ref{sec:results}) establish finite-candidate shortcut \emph{repair}, with
scale/multi-model audits showing it replicates across OpenCLIP backbones. The
non-text semantic-decoy icon benchmark validates a \emph{second} shortcut family,
so the effect is not text-specific. COCO-Text provides dataset-backed
\emph{directional} repair evidence on natural photographs but \emph{not} localization
support: the strict/directional support gates do not pass, and the localization
diagnostic attributes the shortfall to ranking rather than proposal coverage. The
predictive CIC gate (Section~\ref{sec:predgate}) adds a label-free
\emph{abstention} signal that is strongest in pooled/controlled settings and weak on
the hardest COCO-Text subsets. The theorems define the boundary from both sides: the
global conditional theorem states the positive assumptions (proposal completeness,
residual balance) under which repair is shortcut-type-agnostic, while the
no-free-lunch impossibility theorem shows those assumptions are necessary. The
predictive abstention certificate gives the margin-based accept/abstain rule under a
calibrated residual-instability bound. None of these pieces, individually or together,
claims solved open-world discovery, universal/general robustness, pixel-exact
localization, scene-text detection, a passed COCO-Text support gate, an
all-natural-image predictive guarantee, or a guarantee that the method self-certifies
correctness on every input.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
% =====================================================================
CIC separates two questions confidence conflates: \emph{how sure is the model?} and
\emph{is that certainty stable under shortcut interventions?} Confidence answers only
the first; counterfactual stability supplies the second axis. On real pretrained
OpenCLIP, finite-candidate CIC repair of text-overlay shortcuts is validated four
ways: the frozen $n=32$ headline ($0.250\to0.750$ vs.\ $0.331$ matched random), a
full benchmark-resampling audit, a failure-conditioned evaluation, and a scale audit
at $n=128$ that replicates across all four real pretrained OpenCLIP
backbone/checkpoint pairs (gaps $0.437$--$0.778$). The method is not text-specific: a
second, non-text shortcut family --- semantic-decoy icons with no written words ---
passes all gates at $n=64$ and $n=128$ ($0.297$ misleading $\to 0.711$ CIC top-1
vs.\ $0.258$ random). A pooled spatial audit clarifies what the method is: a coarse
causal-intervention tool whose selected regions cover the shortcut and drive repair,
\emph{not} exact localization (median IoU $0.39$). The mechanism is explained not by a
global additive shortcut direction --- which fails because the typographic effect is
object-entangled --- but by the weaker per-input class-balance condition the recovery
theorem actually requires, which is supported. The negative boundaries are equally
clear: the frozen policy does not transfer to a weak-channel watermark shortcut,
WILDS Waterbirds is a real spurious-background diagnostic only, and we claim no
open-world discovery or general robustness. CIC is a finite-candidate counterfactual
intervention framework --- a second reliability axis --- not general shortcut
discovery.
